## 2.1 Force, Mass and Acceleration

Newton's Second Law states that the resultant force acting on an object is directly proportional to its acceleration and inversely proportional to its mass. This relationship is expressed as **F = ma**.

When you push a shopping trolley, the harder you push (greater force), the faster it accelerates. If the trolley is full of items (greater mass), the same push produces a smaller acceleration. This is Newton's Second Law in everyday life.

:::key-terms
**Force (F)** — a push or pull that causes an object to accelerate, measured in newtons (N).

**Mass (m)** — the amount of matter in an object, measured in kilograms (kg). Mass does not change with location.

**Acceleration (a)** — the rate of change of velocity, measured in metres per second squared (m/s²).

**Resultant force** — the single force that represents the combined effect of all forces acting on an object.
:::

:::subject-vocabulary
**Proportional** — when one quantity increases at exactly the same rate as another.

**Inversely proportional** — when one quantity increases as another decreases at the same rate.

**Net force** — another term used for resultant force, commonly used in American textbooks.
:::

## 2.2 Using the Equation F = ma

Applying Newton's Second Law requires careful attention to units and substitution. Always identify what you are given, what you need to find, and select the correct form of the equation.

:::tip
When rearranging *F = ma*, always write out the triangle: Force at the top, Mass and Acceleration at the bottom. Cover the quantity you want to find — what remains shows you the calculation. This avoids rearrangement errors under exam pressure.
:::

:::exam-tip
Examiners award marks for **equation** (1 mark), **correct substitution** (1 mark), and **final answer with units** (1 mark). Always show every step even if you make an arithmetic error — you can still earn the first two method marks.
:::

:::did-you-know
The Saturn V rocket that launched the Apollo missions to the Moon produced a thrust of 35 million newtons — equivalent to the weight of about 2,800 double-decker buses. It accelerated 2.9 million kilograms of spacecraft from zero to 9,920 km/h in just 2.5 minutes.
:::

## 2.3 Applying Newton's Second Law

To solve problems using F = ma, follow a structured approach: identify the known values, select the correct rearrangement, substitute carefully, and state units in your answer.

:::hint
You do not need to memorise the rearranged forms of *F = ma*. Practise rearranging the formula from scratch each time — after a few attempts it becomes automatic. Do not rely on formula triangles in the exam.
:::

:::extend-your-knowledge
Einstein's special theory of relativity reveals that Newton's Second Law breaks down at speeds approaching the speed of light (approximately 3 × 10⁸ m/s). At such speeds, the mass of an object increases with velocity, so the same force produces less and less acceleration. This relativistic effect is observed in particle accelerators such as CERN's Large Hadron Collider, where protons are accelerated to 99.9999991% of the speed of light.
:::

## 2.4 Gravity and Weight

Weight is the gravitational force acting on an object. It is calculated using **W = mg**, where *g* is the gravitational field strength. On Earth's surface, g ≈ 9.8 N/kg. This means a 1 kg mass experiences a downward gravitational force of 9.8 N.

Unlike mass, weight is not constant — it changes depending on the gravitational field strength at your location.

:::maths-skills
**Rearranging W = mg to find mass**

Given: W = 588 N, g = 9.8 N/kg

Step 1 — Write the equation: W = mg

Step 2 — Rearrange for m: m = W ÷ g

Step 3 — Substitute values: m = 588 ÷ 9.8

Step 4 — Calculate: **m = 60 kg**
:::

:::international-context
Gravitational field strength varies across the Earth's surface. At the poles, g ≈ 9.83 N/kg; at the equator, g ≈ 9.78 N/kg. This 0.5% difference has practical consequences — precision weighing instruments used in international pharmaceutical manufacturing must be calibrated for the specific location where they are used to comply with international standards.
:::
