## 3.1 Worked Examples

A **worked example** shows you the full thought process behind a solution, step by step. Follow each stage carefully before attempting the exercises that follow.

:::worked-example
**Example 3.1 — Finding acceleration from force and mass**

A resultant force of 120 N acts on a trolley of mass 40 kg.

**Given:** F = 120 N, m = 40 kg

**Find:** acceleration *a*
:::

:::worked-solution
**Step 1** — Write the equation:

F = ma

**Step 2** — Rearrange for *a*:

a = F ÷ m

**Step 3** — Substitute values:

a = 120 ÷ 40

**Step 4** — State the answer with units:

**a = 3 m/s²**
:::

:::worked-example
**Example 3.2 — Finding force from mass and acceleration**

A 1,200 kg car accelerates from rest at 2.5 m/s².

**Given:** m = 1200 kg, a = 2.5 m/s²

**Find:** resultant force F
:::

:::worked-solution
**Step 1** — Write the equation: F = ma

**Step 2** — Substitute values: F = 1200 × 2.5

**Step 3** — State the answer: **F = 3,000 N**
:::

## 3.2 Activities and Exercises

:::activity
**Investigating Newton's Second Law**

You will need: a dynamics trolley, a runway, masses, a pulley, and a data logger.

1. Set up the runway with the pulley at one end. Attach a string from the trolley over the pulley to a hanging mass.
2. Release the trolley and record the acceleration using the data logger.
3. Repeat the experiment, increasing the hanging mass by 100 g each time, keeping the trolley mass constant.
4. Plot a graph of acceleration (y-axis) against force (x-axis).
5. Describe the relationship your graph shows.

**Safety:** Ensure the runway end is secured so the trolley cannot fall. Keep masses away from feet.
:::

:::exercise
**3.2A — Using F = ma**

For each scenario below, calculate the missing quantity. Show all working and include units.

1. A force of 50 N acts on a 10 kg object. Find the acceleration.
2. An object accelerates at 4 m/s². The resultant force is 200 N. Find the mass.
3. A 750 g ball is kicked and accelerates at 80 m/s². Find the force applied.
4. A 5,000 kg lorry accelerates at 0.8 m/s². What resultant force acts on it?
5. A resultant force of 36 N produces an acceleration of 9 m/s². Find the mass of the object.
:::

## Chapter Summary

:::chapter-summary
**Chapter 3 — Forces and Motion: What you have learned**

- A **force** is a push or pull measured in newtons (N). Forces are vector quantities.
- The **resultant force** is the single force that represents the combined effect of all forces acting on an object.
- **Newton's Second Law**: F = ma — the resultant force equals mass times acceleration.
- **Weight** (W = mg) is the gravitational force on an object. It depends on location; mass does not.
- Gravitational field strength on Earth is approximately 9.8 N/kg.
- Contact forces require physical interaction; non-contact forces (gravity, electrostatic, magnetic) act at a distance.
:::

:::key-points
- F = ma links force (N), mass (kg), and acceleration (m/s²)
- Mass is constant; weight changes with gravitational field strength
- Balanced forces → no change in motion (Newton's First Law)
- A resultant force → acceleration in the direction of that force
- Larger mass → smaller acceleration for the same force
- Gravitational field strength varies slightly across Earth's surface
:::

:::margin-note
Newton published his laws of motion in *Principia Mathematica* in 1687 — written in Latin. It is widely regarded as one of the most important scientific works ever published.
:::
