## 1.1 What is a Force?

A **force** is a push or a pull that acts on an object as a result of its interaction with another object. Forces can change the **shape**, **speed**, or **direction** of motion of an object.

Forces are vector quantities — they have both *magnitude* (size) and *direction*. We measure force in **newtons (N)**, named after Sir Isaac Newton.

When multiple forces act on an object simultaneously, the combined effect is called the **resultant force**. If the resultant force on an object is zero, the forces are described as *balanced*, and the object's motion remains unchanged.

## 1.2 Types of Forces

There are many different types of forces that act in the world around us. Forces can be divided into two broad categories: **contact forces** and **non-contact forces**.

Contact forces require physical contact between two objects. Examples include friction, tension, normal reaction force, and air resistance. These forces are transferred through direct interaction at the point of contact between surfaces.

Non-contact forces can act across empty space without any physical contact. Gravitational force, magnetic force, and electrostatic force are all examples of non-contact forces. The gravitational force between the Earth and the Moon, for instance, acts across approximately 384,000 km of space.

Understanding the distinction between these two categories is fundamental to analysing how objects behave in different physical situations.

## 1.3 Newton's Laws of Motion

Sir Isaac Newton published his three laws of motion in his landmark work *Principia Mathematica* in 1687. These laws form the foundation of classical mechanics and continue to describe the behaviour of everyday objects with remarkable accuracy.

Newton's **First Law** states that an object will remain at rest or continue to move in a straight line at constant speed unless acted upon by a resultant force. This property of matter is called *inertia*.

Newton's **Second Law** states that the resultant force acting on an object is equal to the product of its mass and acceleration. This is expressed as the equation ***F = ma***, where *F* is force in newtons, *m* is mass in kilograms, and *a* is acceleration in metres per second squared.

Newton's **Third Law** states that for every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. When object A exerts a force on object B, object B simultaneously exerts a force of equal magnitude but opposite direction on object A.
